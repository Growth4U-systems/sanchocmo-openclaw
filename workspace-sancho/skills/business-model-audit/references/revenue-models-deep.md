# Revenue Models — Deep Strategy Guide

Detailed marketing strategy per revenue model. Load this reference when the client's model is classified and deeper channel/tactic guidance is needed.

## Subscription SaaS by ACV Tier

### ACV < 5K EUR/year (Volume Play)

**Motion:** PLG pure or PLG + sales-assist at upper limit.

**Channels that work:**
- SEO high-intent (bottom-funnel: "best tool for X", "alternative to [competitor]")
- Paid Search on transactional keywords — Google Ads focused on immediate conversion
- Directories and review sites: G2, Capterra, Product Hunt — these are your salespeople at scale
- Viral educational content: templates, calculators, free tools with built-in virality
- Communities where ICP lives (Reddit, Slack groups, sector Facebook groups)

**Funnel:** Awareness → Free signup/trial → Activation ("aha" moment) → Paid conversion → Seat/feature expansion

**Key metrics:** Time-to-value, activation rate, free-to-paid (benchmark: 3-5% freemium, 25% free trial opt-out), CAC < 200 EUR.

**Common mistake:** Putting an SDR to call $500 ACV leads. The CAC kills unit economics. Automate or die.

### ACV 5-50K EUR/year (PLG-to-Sales)

**Motion:** PLG trial → PQL detection → Sales-assist close. Sales cycles 2-8 weeks.

**Channels that work:**
- Content marketing SEO with vertical comparisons and use cases
- LinkedIn Ads targeting by role + industry — expensive but high lead quality
- Email nurture for non-converting trial users
- Webinars and on-demand demos (reduce sales friction)
- Tech partnerships and integrations (appear in Salesforce, HubSpot marketplace)

**Content that converts:** Case studies with hard numbers, comparison pages vs competitors (high intent), ROI calculators.

**Key metrics:** PQL rate, trial-to-paid, Sales-Assisted vs Self-Serve ratio, CAC 1-3K EUR.

### ACV 50K+ EUR/year (Enterprise)

**Motion:** Enterprise ABM + Field Sales + Executive Sponsorship. Sales cycles 6-12 months. Buying committee of 7-15 people.

**Channels that work:**
- High-touch ABM: personalized content per account, multi-stakeholder air cover
- Analysts (Gartner, Forrester) — a Magic Quadrant placement multiplies enterprise sales
- Exclusive events (CXO dinners, executive roundtables) — access > volume
- Sector PR — enterprise buyers Google your company before the first meeting
- Customer advisory boards — best customers as sellers to prospects

**Content that converts:** Own research/data studies, RFP response templates, analyst reports, customer references.

**Key metrics:** Pipeline coverage (3-5x vs quota), avg deal size, win rate vs specific competitors. CAC can be 15-100K+ EUR but LTV justifies it.

## Transaction / Pay-Per-Use

**Unique challenge:** No "churn" in the classic sense — user simply stops using. No renewal cycle forcing the decision. This changes all marketing psychology.

**Marketing premise:** Sell the result of the first transaction. The goal is that the first experience is so good that repetition is automatic.

**Channels that work:**
- Maximum transactional-intent SEO: user has a need NOW, searches, arrives, uses, pays
- Performance marketing measured to first transaction, not registration
- Marketplace presence where ICP already searches
- Referral programs — incentive can be credit for next transactions
- Integrations — being embedded in the user's workflow

**Critical tactics:**
- Minimize friction on first use. No mandatory registration where possible.
- Post-first-transaction activation email — "What comes next" with additional use cases.
- Volume-escalated pricing creates natural incentive for more transactions.
- API docs and technical documentation AS marketing (if ICP is developers).

**Key metrics:** First transaction rate, repeat transaction rate, transactions/account/month (cohort), revenue per account expansion.

**Common trap:** Measuring CAC against first transaction revenue. In transactional models, unit economics only work looking at 6-12 month cohort LTV.

## Marketplace

**Central paradox:** Seller comes for buyers. Buyer comes for sellers. Need both on day 1.

### Which side first
80% of successful marketplaces prioritized supply first. Without supply, the buyer's first experience is empty = permanent churner.

**Supply-first strategy:**
1. Acquire supply manually (direct calls, 1:1 outreach, high-touch)
2. Subsidize the hard side if necessary (Uber paid early driver salaries)
3. Offer supply something valuable INDEPENDENTLY of demand (OpenTable gave reservation software before being a marketplace)

**Marketing by side:**

| Dimension | Supply Side | Demand Side |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| Primary channel | Direct outreach, sales, LinkedIn | SEO, paid search, social |
| Message | "Earn more / more clients / less friction" | "Find quality X / fair price / trust" |
| Typical CAC | 200-1,000 EUR (3-10x higher) | 20-150 EUR |
| Key metric | # listings, quality score, density per zone | # purchases, retention, repeat rate |

**Anti-chicken-egg tactics:**
- Bring-Your-Own-Demand: supply brings existing clients to the marketplace
- Single-player mode: marketplace offers value with just one side
- Extreme geographic focus: cover one micro-market at 100% before scaling
- Anchor tenants: one high-value supplier attracts demand automatically

## Freemium

**Success vs failure:** Free plan must be genuinely useful (otherwise no one signs up) but must NOT solve 100% of the problem (otherwise no one pays).

**The activation funnel:**
```
Register → Onboarding → Activation ("aha") → Habit formed →
Friction point (free tier limit) → Conversion consideration →
PQL detected → Upgrade
```

**Benchmarks:** Freemium-to-paid: 3-5%. Free trial opt-out to paid: ~50%. Top PLG activation: 20-40%.

**Product-Qualified Leads (PQLs):** Not MQLs. Someone who experienced value and shows purchase intent through usage behavior.

**How to define PQL threshold:** Analyze cohorts of users who DID convert — what did they do differently? Examples: invited a collaborator, exported results, connected an integration, used product Y days in Z weeks.

**Conversion tactics:**
- In-product contextual prompts at the moment user tries a paid feature (not at login)
- Behavioral email nurture (based on actions, not time)
- Progressive limits, not cliff — gradual need, not abrupt wall
- Internal social proof: "3 of your collaborators are already on Pro"
- Trial of Pro plan, not free plan — if you want them to pay, let them experience paid

## Usage-Based / Consumption

**Inverted model:** In classic SaaS, revenue peaks at deal close. In usage-based, revenue grows with use.

**Land-and-expand motion:**
```
Low-friction entry → First use/pilot → Organic expansion by usage →
CSM identifies expansions → Upsell to committed spend / enterprise tier
```

**Expansion marketing tactics:**
- Customer Success as marketing channel (CSM identifies where client could use more)
- Usage dashboards as expansion triggers (show client their usage approaching limits)
- Developer Relations (docs, tutorials, open source, dev conferences) = acquisition AND expansion
- Community-led growth (Slack, Discord, forums where advanced users inspire basic ones)

**Critical metric:** Net Revenue Retention. Median SaaS: 106%. Top performers: >120%. NRR < 100% = shrinking revenue even while acquiring new customers.

## Hybrid (Subscription + Usage)

**2025 data:** 46% of SaaS companies use hybrid models. Hybrid: 21% median growth (higher than pure), 32% higher NDR vs pure subscription.

**Marketing strategies:**
1. Anchor messaging in certainty, not flexibility: "X fixed/month for [core], plus [variable] only if [trigger]"
2. Segment by motion: SMB = highlight low floor (PLG), Enterprise = highlight committed spend discounts (ABM)
3. Communicate "typical spend": "Companies like yours usually spend X-Y/month"
4. Use subscription component as anchor for CAC modeling (fixed = baseline, variable = upside)

## FinTech-Specific Marketing Constraints

### Regulatory (Spain / EU)
- **MiFID II**: Investment marketing content requires risk disclaimers. Mandatory.
- **GDPR/LOPD-GDD**: Stricter than other sectors on tracking, retargeting, audience segmentation.
- **CNMV crypto advertising**: Ads reaching 100K+ people require 10-day advance CNMV notification + risk disclaimer.
- **Google/Meta**: Financial product ads require platform certification + compliance documentation.
- **Practical impact**: Budget 2-4 weeks for compliance review on any new campaign. Legal review of EVERY marketing piece.

### Trust Architecture (hierarchy of impact)
1. Regulation and licenses — "Regulated by [BdE/CNMV/FCA]" with license number. Table stakes.
2. Security certifications — ISO 27001, SOC2, PCI-DSS. Prominent, not footer.
3. Recognizable customer logos — one known bank > 100 unknown SMB testimonials.
4. Transaction volume — "X billion processed" is a massive trust signal.
5. Sector press — El Economista, Expansion, Financial Times.
6. Team credibility — LinkedIn profiles with banking/fintech pedigree.

### Channels for B2B FinTech
1. **LinkedIn** — #1 channel. CFO/CCO/CEO are there. Organic founder content + sponsored targeting.
2. **High-intent SEO** — "crypto compliance platform Spain", "KYC for companies". Low competition, high intent.
3. **Email (own list, not bought)** — GDPR makes mass cold email near-illegal. Own list = gold.
4. **Sector events** — Money20/20, FinTech Connect, AEFI events. B2B fintech buyer values in-person.
5. **Specialized PR** — Finextra, The Paypers, local business press.
6. **Closed communities** — CFO groups, compliance officer circles, treasury manager networks.

**Channels that DON'T work for B2B FinTech:**
- Facebook/Instagram Ads: wrong audience, compliance nightmare
- Mass cold outreach: GDPR + noise = low response
- Traditional influencer marketing: low credibility for B2B financial decisions

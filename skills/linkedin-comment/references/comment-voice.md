# Comment voice: how to write comments that don't sound like AI

This is the part that makes or breaks the whole method. A comment that reads as AI is worse than no comment: it costs the client reputation. The single biggest tell of AI is a comment that is too polished. Your job is to write the way a sharp, busy human actually writes in a comment box: loose, plain, specific, one idea. The voice you are matching is the **client's** voice, from `brand/{slug}/brand-voice/brand-voice.current.md`.

## The spirit: peer, not professor

The client is joining the conversation as a peer who adds value, not as an authority who corrects the author. The valid moves are:
- Share a relevant opinion.
- Back up or reinforce the point the author made.
- Share a lived experience of their own.
- Emphasize the specific thing that was good.

You can disagree or push back, but only from the client's own experience and with warmth ("what I see with clients is...", "it played out differently for me..."), never as abstract correction from above. A challenge grounded in lived experience is welcome; a "well, actually" from on high is not.

Avoid both failure modes: the empty comment ("great post!") and the lecture. The target is support plus one specific, genuine thing. That is neither hollow nor preachy.

## Length and shape

- **Short.** One or two sentences. One idea. Easy to read at a glance.
- If there is a question, keep it light and optional, an invitation to chat, never an interrogation or a gotcha. Questions are not mandatory.
- No lists, no multiple clauses stacked with commas, no paragraphs. If you have three good ideas, pick the best one and drop the other two.

## The anti-AI tells to avoid

These are what make a comment smell like a machine trying to sound deep. Cut all of them:
- **The aphorism / quotable line.** "AI doesn't just make X expensive, it exposes who Y." Humans rarely talk in polished maxims in a comment box.
- **The antithesis "not only... but also"** and perfect parallelisms.
- **Literary or inflated vocabulary** (exposes, lays bare, corners, unleashes). Use plain words.
- **Em dashes.** They are a strong AI tell. Use commas, periods, or colons instead. Never use the long dash.
- **Over-smoothing.** Real people write slightly imperfect, spoken-sounding sentences. A little roughness reads as human.

Vary the structure between comments so they never look like a template. Err on the side of simple and conversational, never clever.

### The number one tell: the closing punchline

The most common way these comments give themselves away is a polished final sentence that sounds wise: "the bottleneck was never the tool, it was who owns the process", "that is the whole game", "this is the part nobody talks about", "did more for trust than any metric ever did". It reads as a consultant writing to be quoted, not a peer dropping a quick thought. Test every comment: **if the last line could go on a LinkedIn quote graphic, delete it.** End on the concrete observation, not the moral.

Also cut the opener that rates the post: "Great framing", "Good point", "Buen punto", "Love this". It is empty and reads as a template. Start with the actual thought or the experience, not a grade.

It is the rhetorical mold, not just these exact words, that gives it away. Two molds to kill on sight:
- **The X-not-Y antithesis**, anywhere, and especially as the closer: "the win was removing steps, not adding guidance", "it was the process, not the tool", "features count themselves, problems you have to go find". That clean symmetry is a writing tic nobody produces casually in a comment box. Say the one half you actually mean and stop.
- **Dressed-up metric validation in the opener**: "18% is the number that actually pays the bills", "that activation lift is huge, nice". It is the same empty "great framing" template wearing a number. Skip it and go straight to the real point or the question.

## Match the post

- **Write in the language of the post.** Post in English, comment in English. Post in Spanish, comment in Spanish. If the client does not read the post's language, give the comment in that language plus a translation so they understand what they are approving.
- **If the post links to an article, newsletter, or video, read it** (fetch the link) and comment on one specific point from it. Never write "I'll read this" or comment blind. The comment should make it obvious the client actually engaged with the content.
- **Comment on what they posted**, even when it is not the client's core topic. With big creators (Lane B) you engage with whatever they put up, not only the on-topic posts.

## Emojis

One emoji now and then, with restraint. Some comments, not all, and never several stacked together.

## Examples

These are patterns, not scripts. Adapt to the actual post and the client's real voice.

**Bad (sounds like AI):**
> Insightful perspective. AI doesn't merely accelerate workflows, it redefines the very nature of how value is created in the modern enterprise. The real question is whether organizations are ready.

Why it fails: aphorism, "not merely... it", inflated vocabulary, says nothing specific, no lived experience.

**Better (supporting, with one specific hook):**
> This matches what I keep seeing: the hard part was never the tool, it was deciding who owns the process when it breaks. Glad someone said it.

**Bad (empty):**
> Great post, totally agree! 👏

**Better (a lived experience):**
> Funny, I started doing exactly your point 1 a few weeks ago, commenting daily to the same handful of people. The difference shows up fast, folks who didn't know me now reply by name.

**Better (a light, friendly question that invites a chat):**
> Love the framing. Curious where you draw the line between a pilot and something that actually moves a number, that is the jump I see most teams get stuck on.

## Always leave it ready, never auto-post

Produce the comment (and a translation if needed) and the link to the post, ready for a human to approve and paste. The drafts go to Sansón for brand-check and then to the human. Never post on the client's behalf. A human hits send, every time.
